Zhijie Wang

王志杰
Ph.D. Student in Environmental Engineering @ UW–Madison

I am a third-year Ph.D. student with a minor in Computer Science at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. My research focuses on mass transfer modeling in membrane systems, with growing interests in scientific machine learning, operator learning, and data-driven methods for PDE-governed transport processes. I also work on Raman spectrum processing and AI-based microscope image segmentation for microplastic detaction.

Prior to my Ph.D., I obtained my Master’s degree in Environmental Engineering from Tongji University, where I focused on membrane processes, electrodialysis, and resource recovery from waste streams. I also completed an Erasmus+ exchange program at the University of Florence and worked as an intern at NOVAMONT S.p.A., gaining international research and industry experience. I received my Bachelor’s degree in Environmental Engineering from Shandong University, where I developed a strong foundation in environmental systems, materials, and biochemical processes.

Research Interests

I work at the intersection of environmental engineering, transport modeling, and scientific machine learning. I am particularly interested in developing physically grounded models that improve how we understand and predict transport behavior in membrane systems. My current direction explores how physics-informed neural networks and Fourier neural networks / neural operators can be used to solve and accelerate membrane transport PDEs.

Scientific Modeling for Membrane Transport Systems

Physical System

Membrane filtration, solute transport, and mass transfer mechanisms.

Mathematical Model

PDE-based descriptions of transport, coupling, and boundary effects.

Learning Framework

PINNs, Fourier neural networks, and operator-learning based surrogates.

Applications

Efficient prediction, scientific understanding, and future engineering design.

About My Work

My research experience spans membrane transport modeling, environmental process design, and materials-related environmental applications. At UW–Madison, I focus on modeling mass transfer in pressure-driven membrane systems, including the transport of amphoteric species such as NH3/NH4+, and developing PDE-based frameworks and alternative physics-informed neural network approaches for solving transport problems. During my master’s research at Tongji University, I worked on membrane bioreactor and electrodialysis systems for value-added chemical recovery from organic waste streams. My work involved studying ion transport, membrane fouling mechanisms, and system-level optimization for resource recovery. I have also conducted research on the degradation of bio-based plastics and anaerobic digestion systems, as well as earlier work on biochar-enhanced methanogenesis and PAH degradation during my undergraduate studies.

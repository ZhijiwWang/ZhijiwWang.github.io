Ph.D. Student in Environmental Engineering @ UW–Madison

I am a third-year Ph.D. student with a minor in Computer Science at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. My research focuses on mass transfer modeling in membrane filtration systems, with growing interests in scientific machine learning, operator learning, and data-driven methods for PDE-governed transport processes.

Research Interests

I work at the intersection of environmental engineering, transport modeling, and scientific machine learning. I am particularly interested in developing physically grounded models that improve how we understand and predict transport behavior in membrane systems. My current direction explores how physics-informed neural networks and Fourier neural networks / neural operators can be used to solve and accelerate membrane transport PDEs.

Scientific Modeling for Membrane Transport Systems

Physical System

Membrane filtration, solute transport, and mass transfer mechanisms.

Mathematical Model

PDE-based descriptions of transport, coupling, and boundary effects.

Learning Framework

PINNs, Fourier neural networks, and operator-learning based surrogates.

Applications

Efficient prediction, scientific understanding, and future engineering design.

About My Work

My broader expertise includes environmental and analytical chemistry, contaminant transport, and computational modeling. I aim to combine domain knowledge with modern machine learning tools to build interpretable and practically useful models for complex environmental systems.

I am currently seeking internship opportunities to further develop and implement scientific machine learning methods, especially PINNs and neural operators, in real-world engineering and data-driven modeling problems.
